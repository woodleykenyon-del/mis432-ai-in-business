    How It Works

    The AI Factory behind CourseFit AI

    CourseFit AI uses a transparent, rules-based scoring system. There is no hidden model or external API. Every point assigned to a recommendation can be traced back to a specific input you provided.

    01 — Data
    Student Inputs
    Major, career goal, interests, completed course areas, and preferred work style — all provided by the student.
    02 — Model
    Scoring and Ranking
    A point-based scoring system compares the student profile against ten course and skill pathways. Each input category contributes up to a fixed number of points.
    03 — Prediction
    Best-Fit Areas
    The system predicts which course or skill areas are most likely to align with the student's career goal and current profile.
    04 — Decision
    Student + Advisor Choice
    The student decides what to explore, research, or discuss with an academic advisor. The prototype supports the decision, it does not make it.
    05 — Value
    Better Planning Conversations
    Students arrive at advising appointments with a clearer shortlist, saving time and helping them ask more targeted questions.
    Business Case

    Why CourseFit AI should exist

    The problem

    Many business students have to choose electives and skill-building paths without a clear view of how those choices connect to their career goals. They often rely on scattered course catalog pages, peer advice, or one-off advising appointments, which can be useful but not always personalized enough. CourseFit AI helps organize that decision by connecting a student's goals, interests, and completed coursework to specific next steps.

    The user

    The target user is a WWU business student, especially a junior or senior choosing electives or trying to prepare for a career path. Before using the prototype, the student may know their major but still feel unsure about which skills or courses will actually help them. After using it, they have a clearer shortlist of options to explore and a stronger starting point for a conversation with an advisor.

    Why this should be AI-powered

    This should be AI-powered because the decision depends on multiple inputs at once: major, career goal, interests, completed coursework, and preferred work style. A spreadsheet or course catalog can list options, but it does not easily translate a student's situation into a personalized recommendation with reasoning. The value of the prototype is not that it replaces an advisor, but that it helps the student ask better questions and narrow the decision before meeting with a human.

    The biggest risk

    The biggest risk is that a student could over-trust the recommendation and avoid a course that is required, time-sensitive, or better suited to their situation than the prototype realizes. For example, a first-generation student trying to graduate on time could be harmed if they treat the tool as official advising and miss a prerequisite or sequencing requirement. That is why CourseFit AI needs a clear human-in-the-loop design: it should support planning, not replace academic advising.

    Course concept 1: Prediction-decision gap

    The prototype predicts which course and skill areas appear to fit a student's profile, but the final decision still belongs to the student and advisor. A recommendation can look strong based on interests and career goals while still being wrong because of prerequisites, graduation timing, financial aid rules, or course availability. That is the prediction-decision gap: a useful prediction does not automatically become the right action.

    Course concept 2: Data dependency

    CourseFit AI is only as good as the information it uses. If the student inputs are incomplete, or if the tool does not include updated course availability, prerequisite chains, or advisor knowledge, its recommendations can become misleading. This is similar to other AI systems from the course where the model's output depends heavily on whether the data actually represents the real decision environment.
