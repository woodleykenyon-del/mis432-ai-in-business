Lab 4

Uber Demand Forecasting, Surge Pricing, and Responsible AI

MIS 432: AI in Business  ·  Western Washington University  ·  Kenyon Woodley

Demand forecasting is the foundation of Uber's operating model: without a reliable picture of where riders will be and when, every downstream decision — driver positioning, pricing, and wait times — is built on guesswork.

Section 01
The Business Problem

Uber does not just move cars around a city reactively. It tries to position supply before demand arrives. That requires a forecasting layer that is always running in the background, estimating where riders will need pickups and when those moments will happen.

Without forecasting, the platform is permanently behind. Drivers end up in the wrong places, wait times climb, riders cancel, and the pricing model reacts to shortages rather than preventing them. The result is worse outcomes for everyone and lower platform efficiency.

📈

Anticipate Demand

Know where riders will be before they open the app, not after the surge has already started.

🚗

Position Drivers

Send drivers to high-demand zones before the rush so supply is in place when the requests hit.

💲

Influence Pricing

Use predicted scarcity to set surge multipliers that attract drivers and dampen excess demand.

⏱

Manage Wait Times

Keep expected wait times short enough that riders stay on the platform instead of walking away.

⚡

Decide in Advance

The best operational decisions happen before the rush, not in the middle of it. Forecasting buys that window.

Operations Memo — Concert Night Recommendation

The model predicts 352 Friday-night trips near campus, with a range of 299 to 405. At a rate of 3 trips per driver per hour, driver need falls between 100 and 135 — against a baseline of 25 drivers typically online. The gap is large under every scenario.

Recommendation: Target approximately 135 drivers near campus at 10pm and activate surge pricing. Plan around the upper bound, not the midpoint, because concert crowds tend to concentrate demand in a short window after the show ends. If the post-show rush is more intense than a normal Friday, actual need could land above even the upper range.

The assumption that concerns me most is that the concert crowd behaves like a normal Friday crowd. A sold-out show ending at 10pm creates a much more concentrated wave than the model was trained on. Treat this as a planning floor, not a ceiling.

Section 04
Stress-Testing the Recommendation

Before committing to the memo, the assumptions behind the forecast were stress-tested. The surge call held across most reasonable scenarios. But the recommendation turned fragile when driver supply assumptions were challenged.

The baseline already implies a serious supply shortfall. What the stress test revealed is that even a good demand forecast becomes operationally weak if drivers simply do not show up in the numbers the model assumes. The real risk on Friday night is not predicting demand wrong — it is assuming supply will respond the way the platform expects.

Section 05
The Algorithm Decides

Uber does not employ someone to read each forecast and decide whether to trigger surge pricing. The prediction feeds directly into a multiplier. No human reviews every decision. The forecast becomes the fare.

This is efficient when context is normal. When a routine Friday night produces more demand than supply, automated surge works exactly as intended. But when context changes, the same logic produces outcomes that feel very different to the people affected by them.

🎶
Concert Ends at 10pm
Demand spikes near the venue. Supply is short. Surge pricing rises to attract drivers and clear the queue. Riders who need a ride pay more; drivers earn more. The market corrects itself over the next 20 minutes.
Automatic surge feels appropriate — market logic applies.
🌋
Earthquake Hits the City
Demand spikes near affected areas. Supply is short. The same algorithm fires the same surge multiplier. Riders trying to evacuate or reach family members face 8x fares. The system cannot distinguish between a concert and a disaster.
Same logic, unacceptable outcome — context changes everything.
Governance Rule — Emergency Surge Override

If emergency-event signals are detected from public safety feeds, local alerts, or an internal incident flag, automatic surge pricing must pause immediately. Any multiplier above base pricing must require human approval before it is shown to riders. This rule is specific enough to implement as a code check and clear enough to support a formal governance policy.

Section 06
Distribution Shift: When the World Changes and the Model Does Not

The Bellingham scenario: Lyft enters the market. Some Uber drivers quietly start splitting their time. The model does not know this. It is still forecasting supply response based on conditions from before Lyft arrived.

Lyft enters Bellingham
Some drivers begin splitting time between platforms. The overall supply available to Uber at any given moment starts declining without any signal in the Uber data.
The model keeps running on old assumptions
Demand forecasts stay accurate. Supply response estimates do not. The model is predicting a city where Uber still has effective control over local driver availability.
Surge misfires and riders wait longer
The algorithm fires surge based on the old supply model. Drivers respond less reliably than expected. Riders wait longer. The system acts confident in decisions that are based on a market that no longer exists.
Drivers chase outdated demand patterns
Positioning recommendations still reflect old demand geography. Drivers following the app end up in the wrong places, earning less than expected and reinforcing the supply shortfall.
Error rises — silently
Forecast errors compound week over week. Without a monitoring threshold, the model continues running without triggering any review. The bias grows into the next training cycle.
Governance Policy for Distribution Shift

An AI governance policy for this model needs clear ownership, defined monitoring thresholds, scheduled review of forecast error, and authority for someone to pause or retrain the model when error rises sharply. The accountable person must be close enough to both operations and modeling to recognize what distribution shift looks like in real-world pricing and driver decisions.

The rule: if forecast error exceeds a defined threshold for a sustained period, the model cannot keep running silently without review.

Section 07
What It Feels Like From the Inside

In the driver simulation, the signal that something was wrong was not one bad number. It was the mismatch between what the app suggested and what earnings and rider outcomes actually looked like. The platform still appeared confident while the logic had already stopped working.

The Problem
Misaligned Signals
Demand signals and multipliers stopped lining up with actual outcomes around round 5. Drivers following the platform's guidance moved toward demand that was no longer concentrated where the algorithm thought it was.
The Effect
Wasted Movement, Lower Earnings
Drivers spent time and fuel repositioning based on outdated signals. Earnings came in below what the multiplier implied. Riders in outer areas like Cordata experienced worse service even though the algorithm was still acting confident.
The Trust Problem
Algorithms Shape Income
The platform is not just recommending routes. It is shaping what drivers earn. If a driver cannot understand why demand signals, multipliers, or payouts are shifting, it becomes rational to stop trusting and responding to the platform.
The Feedback Loop
Trust Failure Weakens the Model
Once driver trust breaks down, supply becomes less predictable. That unpredictability feeds directly back into the forecasting system, because the model becomes less reliable when drivers stop responding predictably to positioning signals.

Step 1 — Exploration
Why does the school-year effect matter most for the concert forecast?
The concert is happening near campus during the school year, so student presence changes the baseline before the concert spike is even added. If the model misses how much busier campus Fridays are when WWU is in session, it will undershoot demand before accounting for the concert crowd at all. Skipping exploration would have meant missing that near-campus Friday demand behaves differently depending on the academic calendar.
Step 2 — Backtesting
Why can you not skip the backtest?
Without a backtest you have no idea whether the model can predict anything reasonable on data it has never seen. The backtest is what tells you whether the model understands the pattern or is just fitting the past in a way that falls apart immediately. Skipping it means you could still produce a forecast number, but you would have no way to know if that number was useful, fragile, or completely misleading.
Step 3 — Forecast Output
How would you frame this for your boss?
The forecast is useful, but it should be treated as a planning tool rather than a hard fact. The prediction interval matters here because concert nights are exactly the kind of situation where demand can run hot in a short window. Frame the driver recommendation around the upper bound rather than the midpoint, and make clear that if the post-show rush is more concentrated than normal, actual need could land above even the upper range.
Step 4 — Stress Testing
What does the stress test actually reveal?
The surge call stayed on in most reasonable scenarios, which means the baseline forecast already implies real supply pressure. The more revealing finding was that driver availability assumptions had the biggest effect on the recommendation — bigger than demand level uncertainty. The real risk is not predicting demand wrong. It is assuming supply will show up the way the model expects.
Step 5 — Context vs. Algorithm
What actually differs between concert and earthquake?
Not the algorithm. The context. In the concert scenario, surge is doing what it was designed to do in a normal market situation where demand temporarily spikes. In the earthquake scenario, the same logic runs on a public safety problem, and our expectations of what the system should be allowed to do are completely different. The algorithm has no way to know which world it is in.
Step 6 — Distribution Shift
What would you add to this system for a real deployment?
A stronger real-time event signal so the model could recognize unusual nights faster, rather than relying entirely on historical patterns. And a monitoring layer with clear ownership — a specific person accountable for watching forecast error and empowered to pause or retrain the model when something looks wrong. Distribution shift is only dangerous if no one is watching for it.
